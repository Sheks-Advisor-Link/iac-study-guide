● ACC — Financial Accounting Summary Condensed from your Endunamoo ACC pack — every high-yield IFRS with a layman explainer

Conceptual Framework 2018

The five "elements" and their recognition

Elements: Asset, Liability, Equity, Income, Expense.

Asset: Present economic resource controlled by the entity as a result of past events, capable of producing economic benefits.
Liability: Present obligation arising from past events, settlement of which is expected to result in an outflow.
Equity: Residual interest in assets after deducting liabilities.

Recognition criteria: only recognise if it provides users with relevant information and a faithful representation — considering existence uncertainty and measurement uncertainty.

An "asset" is something you control that will make you money. A "liability" is something you owe that will cost you money. You only put it on the balance sheet if it's real enough AND measurable enough to be useful to someone reading the statements.
Qualitative characteristics

Fundamental: Relevance + Faithful representation.

Enhancing: Comparability · Verifiability · Timeliness · Understandability.

Constraint: Cost must not exceed benefit.

Numbers must (1) matter to the decision (relevance) and (2) actually reflect reality (faithful representation). The other four make it easier to use. And don't spend a fortune producing info nobody needs.

IAS 1 — Presentation of Financial Statements

Complete set of AFS: SFP, SP/L&OCI, SCE, SCF, Notes, (comparatives), opening SFP if retrospective restatement.

Going concern assessment is mandatory. Disclose material uncertainties.

Offsetting is not permitted unless IFRS requires/permits it.

Five statements + notes. Always assume the business keeps trading unless there's serious doubt — if there is, flag it loudly in the notes. Don't net assets against liabilities.

IAS 2 — Inventories

Measure at lower of cost and NRV. Cost = purchase + conversion + other costs to bring to present location & condition. Exclude abnormal waste, storage (unless production), admin, selling.

NRV = estimated selling price less costs to complete and costs to sell.

Formulas allowed: FIFO or Weighted Average. LIFO is prohibited.

If selling price has dropped below cost → write down to NRV through P/L. If NRV later recovers → reverse up to original cost (but not above).

IAS 8 — Accounting Policies, Changes in Estimates & Errors

HOT — you lost marks here in June

Change in accounting policy → apply retrospectively. Restate comparatives. Adjust opening RE.

Change in accounting estimate → apply prospectively. No restatement.

Prior period error → retrospectively restate. Correct the comparative P/L, restate opening SFP of earliest period presented, adjust opening retained earnings.

Prior period error — exam structure (memorise this order)

  1. Correct the prior-year P/L (restate the comparative)
  2. Correct the prior-year SFP (restate comparative balances)
  3. Adjust opening retained earnings of the earliest period presented
  4. Disclose nature of error, correction amounts per line item, EPS if applicable
Messed up last year? Pretend it was always right. Redo last year's numbers, bring the fix into opening retained earnings, and explain what happened in the notes.

IAS 12 — Income Taxes (Deferred Tax)

Temporary differences

Taxable TD (CA > TB for asset, or CA < TB for liability) → DT Liability
Deductible TD (CA < TB for asset, or CA > TB for liability) → DT Asset

DT = Temporary difference × future applicable tax rate
Exchange: DT liability = (CA − TB) × tax rate (for an asset)

Exception: no DT on initial recognition of goodwill.

DT Asset — only recognise to extent probable taxable profits will be available.

Deferred tax smooths out tax timing differences. If the taxman lets you deduct something faster than accounting does, you'll pay more tax later — that's a deferred tax liability now. Vice versa for an asset.

IAS 16 — Property, Plant & Equipment

Recognition, measurement, depreciation

Recognise if probable future economic benefits AND cost can be reliably measured.

Initial: cost = purchase price + directly attributable + dismantling obligation (PV).

Subsequent: Cost model OR Revaluation model (by class).

Depreciation: systematic basis over useful life, from when asset is available for use. Review residual, useful life, method annually.

Component approach — major components depreciated separately.

If an asset is "available for use" but not yet in production, depreciation STILL starts. Mark-losing confusion — don't wait for first output.

IAS 36 — Impairment of Assets

Recoverable amount = higher of FV less costs of disposal and Value in use.

Impairment loss = CA − Recoverable amount (if CA > RA).

Indicators to test: external (market value decline, adverse economic changes) and internal (obsolescence, physical damage, restructuring).

Annual testing (even no indicators): goodwill, intangibles with indefinite life, intangibles not yet available for use.

Reversal: allowed for everything EXCEPT goodwill.

If an asset is worth less than its book value — write it down. Test goodwill every year regardless. If things improve, you can reverse (except goodwill — gone is gone).

IAS 37 — Provisions, Contingent Liabilities & Contingent Assets

Provision recognised when:
1. Present obligation (legal or constructive) from past event
2. Probable (>50%) outflow
3. Reliable estimate possible

Contingent liability = disclosed only (possible obligation OR probable but can't measure).

Contingent asset = disclosed only if probable; recognised only when virtually certain.

Measurement: best estimate of expenditure required at reporting date, discounted if time value material.

Onerous contract — unavoidable cost > benefits. Recognise provision = lower of (cost of fulfilling) vs (cost of terminating).

IAS 38 — Intangible Assets HOT

Definition: Identifiable, non-monetary asset without physical substance, controlled by entity, future economic benefits expected.

Research vs Development

  • Research phase: ALWAYS expense. Future benefits too uncertain.
  • Development phase: capitalise if and only if ALL 6 criteria met.

The 6 Development Capitalisation Criteria — "PIRATE"

  1. Probable future economic benefits
  2. Intention to complete and use/sell
  3. Resources (technical, financial, etc.) adequate to complete
  4. Ability to use or sell
  5. Technical feasibility of completing
  6. Expenditure reliably measurable
The recognition date = latest date at which ALL 6 criteria are met. Costs incurred BEFORE that date must be expensed and cannot be reinstated later. This is the #1 mark-losing trap.

Amortisation

Finite life → amortise over useful life. Indefinite life → no amortisation, annual impairment test.

What CANNOT be capitalised (internally generated):

Brands, mastheads, publishing titles, customer lists, goodwill.

Money spent "figuring out if something works" = research = expense it. Once you've proved it works AND you can afford to finish it AND you'll sell/use it AND you can measure what you've spent → then you can capitalise it as an asset. But only from the day ALL those boxes are ticked.

IFRS 3 — Business Combinations HOT

Business combination = acquirer obtains control of one or more businesses (not just assets).

Acquisition date = date acquirer obtains control (usually when consideration transferred and acquirer takes control).

Acquisition method — 4 steps:

  1. Identify the acquirer
  2. Determine the acquisition date
  3. Recognise and measure identifiable assets acquired, liabilities assumed, NCI
  4. Recognise and measure goodwill (or bargain purchase gain)

IFRS 9 — Financial Instruments (deep dive)

Scope

Applies to all financial assets (FA) and financial liabilities (FL) unless specifically excluded (leases → IFRS 16; subsidiaries → IFRS 10; employee benefits → IAS 19; insurance → IFRS 17).

Step 1 — Classification of FINANCIAL ASSETS

Two tests run in order:

(1) Business Model (BM) test — how does the entity manage these FAs to generate cash?
(2) SPPI test — are contractual cash flows Solely Payments of Principal & Interest?
Interest = consideration for the time value of money + credit risk + other basic lending risks/costs + profit margin.

Business ModelSPPI?ClassificationMeasurement
Hold to collectYesAmortised CostEffective interest method, ECL
Hold to collect & sellYesFVOCI — debtFV changes → OCI; interest, ECL, FX → P/L. On derecognition, OCI recycled to P/L.

Step 2 — Classification of FINANCIAL LIABILITIES

  • Default: Amortised Cost using effective interest rate (EIR)
  • Exceptions FVTPL: held for trading, derivatives, designation (fair-value option) where it eliminates an accounting mismatch
  • Own credit risk on FVTPL-designated liabilities → presented in OCI (not P/L)

Step 3 — Initial measurement

At FAIR VALUE. Transaction costs:

  • FVTPL → expensed immediately
  • Other categories → added to (FA) / deducted from (FL) initial carrying amount

Step 4 — Subsequent measurement (Amortised cost — worked)

CAt+1 = CAt + Interest (EIR × CAt) − Cash received/paid

The EIR is the rate that exactly discounts estimated future cash flows to the initial net carrying amount (so if there are transaction costs, the EIR ≠ coupon rate).

Step 5 — Expected Credit Loss (ECL) — the 3-stage model

StageWhenECLInterest
1 — PerformingNo significant increase in credit risk since initial recognition12-month ECLEIR × Gross CA
2 — UnderperformingSignificant increase in credit risk (but not yet impaired)Lifetime ECLEIR × Gross CA

Simplified approach (mandatory) for trade receivables, contract assets and lease receivables (policy choice for lease) → always lifetime ECL. No staging. Use a provision matrix: historic default rates by age bucket × forward-looking adjustment.

ECL = PD × LGD × EAD
PD = probability of default
LGD = loss given default (1 − recovery %)
EAD = exposure at default (usually gross CA)

Step 6 — Derecognition

FA derecognised when: (a) contractual rights expire OR (b) rights transferred AND substantially all risks/rewards transferred OR (c) control transferred even if risks/rewards don't.

FL derecognised when the obligation is discharged, cancelled or expires. Substantial modification (>10% PV test) = new liability, old extinguished; difference → P/L.

Step 7 — Hedge Accounting (overview)

Three types: Fair-value hedge (hedge FV exposure), Cash-flow hedge (hedge variability of future CFs), Net investment hedge (foreign operation).

FV hedge: both hedged item and hedging instrument FV changes → P/L.
CF hedge: effective portion → OCI, ineffective portion → P/L. Recycle OCI when hedged CF affects P/L.
NI hedge: effective → OCI (FCTR), recycled on disposal.

Qualifying criteria: formal designation & documentation · economic relationship · credit risk doesn't dominate · hedge ratio reflects actual quantities.

Exam triggers for IFRS 9

  • "Significant increase in credit risk" — argue qualitatively (payments 30+ days past due is a rebuttable presumption)

IFRS 2 — Share-Based Payment (deep dive)

Applies whenever an entity receives goods/services in exchange for its equity instruments or cash amounts based on its share price. Three types:

  1. Equity-settled — entity issues own shares/options
  2. Cash-settled — pays cash based on share price (SARs — Share Appreciation Rights)
  3. Choice of settlement — either the counterparty or entity has the choice

Measurement principle

Cash-settledFV of liabilityEach reporting date AND settlement dateYES — remeasured every reporting date through P/L

Vesting conditions

  • Service conditions — employee must remain for a period → factor into number expected to vest
  • Non-market performance conditions (EPS, profit targets) → factor into number expected to vest; revise each year
  • Market conditions (share price targets, TSR) → baked into grant-date FV at inception; no subsequent revision even if not met
  • Non-vesting conditions (employee has to contribute, or not do something) → baked into grant-date FV

Cumulative expense = FV at grant date × Estimated number expected to vest × (Vested period elapsed / Total vesting period)

Period expense = Cumulative expense this year − Cumulative expense last year

IFRS 11 — Joint Arrangements (deep dive)

Replaced IAS 31. Applies where two or more parties have joint control — contractually-agreed sharing of control, decisions about relevant activities require the unanimous consent of the parties sharing control.

Two types of joint arrangement

Decision tree (how to classify)

  1. Is there a separate vehicle? NO → Joint Operation.
  2. YES — does the legal form of the vehicle give parties rights to net assets? Assess:
    • Legal form of the separate vehicle
    • Terms of the contractual arrangement
    • Other facts and circumstances (e.g. output sold only to parties, financing dependent on parties)
  3. If parties have rights to the NET ASSETS → JV. If parties have rights to specific assets and obligations for liabilities → JO.

Accounting mechanics — Joint Operation

Joint operator recognises its share of:

  • Assets (including share of jointly-held assets)
  • Liabilities (including share of jointly-incurred liabilities)
  • Revenue from output
  • Expenses (including share of jointly-incurred expenses)

Eliminate profits on transactions with the JO to the extent of your interest.

IFRS 10 — Consolidated Financial Statements

Control exists when an investor has ALL of:

  1. Power over the investee (rights that give ability to direct relevant activities)
  2. Exposure/rights to variable returns
  3. Ability to use power to affect those returns

Intra-group items eliminate in full. Unrealised profits in inventory/PPE eliminate (with DT).

If you control another company (not just own shares), consolidate it — line-by-line add everything, then scrub out all the in-family transactions.

IFRS 15 — Revenue from Contracts with Customers

5-step model

  1. Identify the contract
  2. Identify the performance obligations
  3. Determine the transaction price
  4. Allocate the transaction price to the performance obligations
  5. Recognise revenue when (or as) the entity satisfies a performance obligation

Point-in-time vs over-time: over-time if customer simultaneously receives/consumes OR entity's performance creates/enhances customer-controlled asset OR no alternative use + enforceable right to payment for work done.

Principal vs Agent: Principal controls the good/service before transferring → gross revenue. Agent arranges → net commission.

Unpack every arrangement into discrete performance obligations. Marks come from identifying each one separately and allocating price to each. Don't treat a bundle as one PO if components are distinct.

IFRS 16 — Leases HOT

Lessee accounting — single model

Recognise a Right-of-Use Asset (ROU) and a Lease Liability for almost every lease.

Lease Liability (initial) = PV of lease payments, discounted at rate implicit in lease (or IBR if not determinable)

ROU Asset (initial) = Lease Liability + prepayments + initial direct costs + dismantling provision − lease incentives received

Subsequent measurement

Lease liability: amortised cost — interest accrued, lease payments reduce balance.
ROU asset: cost less accumulated depreciation & impairment. Depreciate over shorter of useful life / lease term (unless ownership transfers or purchase option expected to exercise — then useful life).

Two exemptions (lessee may elect)

  • Short-term leases: ≤12 months, no purchase option
  • Low-value leases: the underlying asset is low value when new (IASB guidance: ~US$5,000). Assessed on an asset-by-asset basis, regardless of materiality to entity.
Interdependent assets = NO low-value exemption. If the asset can only be used together with other assets (e.g. individual racks of a warehousing system, a leased laptop that's part of a networked system), the "low value" test fails. You must capitalise ROU and liability. This is the exact area you lost marks on.

Lease correction journal (when an error is discovered)

If the lease was incorrectly expensed (not capitalised):

Dr ROU Asset (at what CA should now be)
Cr Lease Liability (at what balance should now be)
Dr/Cr Retained Earnings (prior-period difference, net of tax)
Dr/Cr Deferred Tax (tax effect of the adjustment)
Under IFRS 16, if you rent anything longer than a year and it's not a tiny item, you treat it as BOTH buying the asset (ROU) AND owing the debt (lease liability). The exemptions are for pure short rentals and genuinely cheap individual items. If the thing only works as part of a bigger setup, you can't claim it's "low value" on its own.

📊 Consolidations — Full Worked Walkthrough IFRS 10 + IFRS 3 combined — the exact mechanics of a group AFS question

Why consolidation questions lose marks

Consolidation questions are predictable but mark-dense. Marks are lost on the structure, not the concepts:

  1. Forgetting the at-acquisition FV adjustments and their DT effect
  2. Mixing up NCI at FV vs at proportionate share
  3. Failing to eliminate unrealised profit (and the associated DT)
  4. Wrongly attributing profit between Parent shareholders and NCI
  5. Forgetting to eliminate intra-group dividends

Step-by-step consolidation framework

  1. Determine control (IFRS 10 3-element test)
  2. Identify acquisition date (when control is obtained)
  3. Measure consideration transferred at fair value
  4. Measure NCI — at FV or at proportionate share of identifiable net assets (policy choice per acquisition)
  5. Fair-value identifiable assets and liabilities (recognise intangibles and contingent liabilities per IFRS 3 even if not on S's books)
  6. Compute DT on FV adjustments at 27%
  7. Compute goodwill = [Consid + NCI + FV prev held] − FV net assets
  8. Post pro-forma journals at year-end: (a) elimination of investment & at-acquisition equity, (b) since-acquisition movements, (c) intra-group transactions, (d) unrealised profit + DT, (e) dividend elimination
  9. Attribute profit & equity between Parent shareholders and NCI

The full worked example — "Parent P Ltd / Sub S Ltd"

Facts

  • P acquired 80% of S on 1 January 20X1 for R12,000,000 cash
  • At acquisition date, S's equity: Share capital R4,000,000; Retained earnings R5,000,000
  • FV adjustments at acquisition:
    • PPE: carrying amount R3m, FV R5m (remaining useful life 10 years, no residual)
    • Previously unrecognised patent: FV R1m (useful life 5 years)
  • NCI measured at FV: R2,400,000
  • Tax rate: 27%
  • During 20X1:

Associate (IAS 28) — Equity method overview

Applied where investor has significant influence (generally 20–50% holding; can be less with board representation etc.).

Cost of investment (start)
+ Share of post-acq profit/(loss) after-tax
+ Share of OCI movements
− Dividends received from associate

Acquisition mid-year

If P acquires S mid-year:

  • Only consolidate S's results from the date of acquisition
  • Time-apportion S's annual profit → pre-acq vs post-acq

Audit planning & risk (ISA 300, 315, 320, 330)

Audit risk model

Audit Risk = Inherent Risk × Control Risk × Detection Risk
(ROMM = IR × CR)

Auditor assesses IR and CR (they exist in the client). Auditor controls DR by the nature, timing and extent of procedures.

Materiality

Overall: benchmark × %. Typical benchmarks:
· 5% of profit before tax (profit-oriented)
· 1% of revenue (loss-making or volatile profit)
· 1–2% of total assets (asset-intensive)
· 1% of total equity (investment entities)

Performance materiality = typically 50–75% of overall materiality, set to reduce risk of undetected misstatement exceeding overall materiality.

Clearly trivial = typically 5% of overall — below this no need to accumulate.

Materiality is "how big an error do we care about?" Performance materiality is the lower bar we work to during the audit so that lots of small things don't add up to a big thing.

Sufficient appropriate audit evidence

Sufficient = quantity. Appropriate = quality (relevance + reliability).

Reliability hierarchy (most → least reliable)

Companies Act — Directors' duties (s75, 76, 77) HOT

Section 75 — Disclosure of personal financial interests

A director with a personal financial interest in a matter: disclose the interest and its general nature → recuse themselves from board discussion and voting → not be regarded as present for quorum on that matter.

Section 76 — Standards of directors' conduct

  • Act in good faith and for a proper purpose
  • Act in the best interests of the company
  • Act with the degree of care, skill and diligence that may reasonably be expected of (a) a person carrying out the same functions AND (b) having the general knowledge, skill and experience of that director
  • Not use position / information for personal gain or to knowingly cause harm

Business judgment rule (s76(4))

A director satisfies their duty of care if:

  1. Took reasonable steps to become informed
  2. Had no personal financial interest (or disclosed per s75)
  3. Made a decision or supported it in good faith for a proper purpose with a rational basis

Section 77 — Liability of directors

A director is personally liable for any loss, damages or costs sustained by the company as a consequence of breach of fiduciary duty (s76), breach of the Act, reckless trading (s22), false statements, etc.

Got skin in the game? Tell the board and step out. Always act like a reasonable, informed person doing their job honestly for the company. Mess it up recklessly → you personally pay.

How to write a substantive procedure (mark-by-mark)

Every procedure must contain 4 elements. Miss any one = lose the mark:

  1. Assertion being addressed (existence, completeness, etc.)
  2. Verb — a concrete audit action (inspect, recalculate, confirm, observe, agree, trace, enquire)
  3. Source of evidence — specific document or record
  4. Compare to — what you're agreeing/checking against

Template: "[Verb] the [source] and [compare/agree/trace] to the [target] to verify [assertion]."

Example: "Inspect the signed sales invoice and agree the quantity and price to the underlying GRN and approved price list, to verify the accuracy of revenue."

Gross Income (s1 definition)

"The total amount, in cash or otherwise, received by or accrued to or in favour of a resident from anywhere in the world (non-resident: SA source only), during a year of assessment, excluding receipts of a capital nature."

6 elements to prove it's gross income

  1. Total amount
  2. In cash or otherwise
  3. Received by / accrued to
  4. Resident (worldwide) / non-resident (SA source)
  5. During year of assessment
  6. NOT of a capital nature

Plus the special inclusions (par (a)–(n)) — e.g. annuities, alimony, services rendered, restraint of trade payments, leasehold improvements, key-person insurance etc., which are included even if capital in nature.

Capital vs Revenue HOT

Not defined in the Act — relies on case law. Ask:

  1. Intention at acquisition (income-producing tree vs fruit)?
  2. Change of intention — did it become trading stock?
  3. Scheme of profit-making (CIR v Pick 'n Pay Employee Share Trust)?
  4. Nature of asset — fixed vs floating
  5. Frequency of similar transactions
  6. Holding period
  7. Manner of disposal

Classic cases

  • CIR v Visser — "tree vs fruit" test
  • CIR v Stott — intention at acquisition
  • Natal Estates v SIR — change of intention
  • CIR v Pick 'n Pay Employee Share Trust — scheme of profit-making
Capital = the money-making machine. Revenue = the money coming out of it. Selling the machine = capital. Selling what the machine makes = revenue. If you bought it to resell at a profit → revenue. If you bought it to use and it just happened to get sold → capital. Intention + what you did + why = the answer.

General deduction formula — s11(a) read with s23(g)

s11(a) — positive test

"Expenditure and losses actually incurred in the production of income, provided they are not of a capital nature."

s23(g) — negative test

"No deduction for moneys claimed to the extent that they are not laid out or expended for the purposes of trade."

Requirements (tick ALL)

  1. Expenditure or loss
  2. Actually incurred (unconditional legal liability — Edgars Stores)
  3. During the year of assessment
  4. In the production of income (Port Elizabeth Electric Tramway — close connection)
  5. Not of a capital nature (New State Areas — enduring benefit test)

Applied when a taxpayer receives income in advance under a contract, and the related expenditure will only be incurred in future years.

Requirements

  1. Income received/accrued must be included in gross income this year
  2. Must be under a contract (the income one and the expense one typically the same contract — Big G v SARS)
  3. Future expenditure must be incurred in a later year in the performance of the taxpayer's obligations under that contract
  4. Future expenditure must be allowable as a deduction when incurred (i.e. would satisfy s11)

Calculation

s24C allowance = (Future expenditure under contract) × (Income received in advance ÷ Total contract income)

= Proportion of advance receipt that matches future cost

Treatment

  • Allowed as deduction in current year (reverses in the next year)
  • Maximum = future allowable expenditure under the contract
Customer pays you R1m upfront for a job you'll only finish next year. SARS doesn't want to tax you on the full R1m now if you still have R700k of costs to spend later. s24C lets you defer tax on the portion matching future costs.
Big G case: the income contract and the expense contract must be the same contract (or very closely linked). Don't apply s24C if the future costs are under a different arrangement.

Trading stock — s22 HIGH

Taxable income impact = Closing stock − Opening stock − Purchases (as three separate lines).

Valuation

  • Closing stock valued at lower of cost OR market value
  • Diminution (just and reasonable) allowed under s22(1)(a)
  • Obsolete / damaged stock → write down to lower value

Donations of trading stock

  • Add to closing stock at cost under s22(8) OR recognise deemed receipt
  • Donation to a PBO with s18A receipt → may claim s18A deduction up to 10% of taxable income

Private / non-trade use

Deemed recoupment at market value (s22(8)).

Closing stock value affects your profit. If it's worth less than you paid → write it down (reduces profit, reduces tax). If you give stock away for free → you lose the deduction unless it's to a registered public benefit org.

Variable remuneration — s7B HIGH

Bonuses, commission, leave pay, overtime etc. are taxed (and deductible) on actual payment basis — NOT when accrued.

Exam trap: company accrues a R500k bonus at year-end 28 Feb, pays in March. For the employer, the deduction is in the following year when paid. Same for the employee's inclusion in gross income.

Restraint of trade HIGH

Recipient (taxpayer restrained)

Full amount included in gross income in year of receipt (special inclusion para (cA) / (cB)).

Payer

Deductible under s11(cA):

  • Over the period of restraint OR
  • Over 3 years, whichever is longer
You pay an ex-employee R900k to not compete for 2 years. They include the full R900k in gross income year 1. You spread the deduction over 3 years (R300k/year) — the longer of "period" (2) or "minimum 3".

Dividends HIGH

Local dividends

Received by SA resident company from SA resident company → exempt under s10(1)(k).
Subject to Dividends Tax (DT) of 20% withheld by the paying company — but exemptions apply (including resident company shareholder).

Foreign dividends

Partially exempt under s10B:

Exempt portion = Foreign dividend × 25/45 (natural person) or 8/27 (company)
Effect: an effective rate of ~20% (individual) or ~20% (company)

OR fully exempt if participation exemption applies (≥10% of equity shares & voting rights in foreign company).

Common trap: students include a foreign dividend at full value OR apply the local dividend exemption. Neither is correct — compute the partial exemption precisely, or confirm participation exemption.

● MAF — Managerial Accounting & Finance Summary Cost of capital, valuations, NPV, capital structure, strategy — with plain-English explainers

Cost of Capital — WACC

WACC = (E/V) × Ke + (D/V) × Kd × (1 − t)

Where:
E = Market value of equity
D = Market value of debt
V = E + D
Ke = Cost of equity (from CAPM)
Kd = Pre-tax cost of debt (YTM of bonds)
t = Tax rate (27% for companies)

Use market values, not book values

Market values reflect what investors currently demand; book values are historical.

Cost of equity — CAPM

Ke = Rf + β × (Rm − Rf)

Rf = risk-free rate (long-dated government bond, e.g. R186)
β = beta of the share (systematic risk)
(Rm − Rf) = equity risk premium (typically 5–7% SA)

Cost of debt

Use yield to maturity on existing debt, NOT the coupon rate. Use current market rate for new debt.

WACC is the minimum return a company must earn to satisfy ALL investors. Debt is cheaper than equity (tax-deductible, less risky), so more debt lowers WACC — until financial distress kicks in.

WACC critiques (what to fault) HOT

Classic exam question: "Critique this WACC calculation." Walk through:

  1. Risk-free rate — is the term matching the project's life? (10-yr project → 10-yr bond, not 91-day T-bill)
  2. Beta — is it the right beta?
    • Listed company: can observe directly
    • Unlisted or different project: "pure play" — use a similar listed company's beta, unlever & relever for target capital structure
    • Hamada: βL = βU × [1 + (1−t)(D/E)]
  3. Equity risk premium — consistent & supportable (not company's past returns)
  4. Capital structure weights — use TARGET weights for project evaluation, not current snapshot if it's unstable
  5. Cost of debt — is this YTM of new debt, or old coupon?
  6. Tax rate — marginal rate, not effective
  7. Project risk ≠ company risk — for a very different project, WACC is not appropriate; risk-adjust
"Valuation critique" answers always follow this: for each input, state what it IS, say what it SHOULD be, quantify if possible, give a recommendation. Six bullet-style points = 6 marks.

NPV — Net Present Value HOT

NPV = Σ [CFt / (1+r)^t] − Initial Investment

Decision rule:
NPV > 0 → ACCEPT (creates shareholder value)
NPV < 0 → REJECT
NPV = 0 → Indifferent

IRR — Internal Rate of Return

The discount rate at which NPV = 0. Accept if IRR > WACC.

Limitations: multiple IRRs possible when CF signs flip multiple times; reinvestment rate assumption; doesn't scale for size. When IRR and NPV conflict, NPV wins.

Company Valuation approaches

1. DCF (FCFF)

Value of operations = Σ [FCFF / (1+WACC)^t] + Terminal Value

FCFF = EBIT × (1−t) + Dep − Capex − ΔWC

Terminal Value (Gordon) = FCFFt+1 / (WACC − g)

Equity value = Value of operations − Net debt

2. DCF (FCFE)

FCFE = Net income + Dep − Capex − ΔWC + Net borrowing
Value of equity = Σ [FCFE / (1+Ke)^t] + TV

3. Relative valuation (multiples)

P/E, EV/EBITDA, P/B. Pick comparable companies; apply industry median multiple.

4. Net asset value

Balance sheet approach — often gives floor value.

DCF = "what will it earn, brought back to today." Relative = "what do similar companies sell for?" NAV = "what would we get breaking it up?" Never rely on one — triangulate.

Capital structure

Modigliani-Miller Proposition I (no tax): capital structure irrelevant — firm value independent of debt/equity mix.

MM with tax: more debt = higher value (tax shield) — theoretically 100% debt is optimal.

Trade-off theory: optimal D/E balances tax shield benefit vs financial distress cost.

Pecking order: firms prefer internal funds → debt → equity (due to signalling).

Key ratios

  • Debt-to-equity
  • Interest cover (EBIT/Interest) — should be > 3x comfortably
  • Debt service cover

Dividend policy

Theories: irrelevance (MM) · bird-in-hand · tax-preference · signalling · residual.

Types of distribution: cash dividend · scrip (bonus shares) · share buy-back · special dividend.

Considerations: cash availability · debt covenants · investment opportunities · tax · signal to market.

Cash Conversion Cycle = Inventory Days + Debtor Days − Creditor Days

Debtor days = Debtors / Revenue × 365

1. IAS 38 — Intangibles (recognition date trap)

Worked example. A company spent:

  • 1 Jan – 30 Jun: R400,000 investigating whether a new herbicide works (research)
  • 1 Jul: technical feasibility demonstrated
  • 1 Jul – 30 Sep: R300,000 spent — but no formal plan yet
  • 1 Oct: management approved the budget + signed off on intention, ability, and market
  • 1 Oct – 31 Dec: R500,000 spent completing development

Capitalisation date = 1 October (the latest date on which all 6 PIRATE criteria were simultaneously met). Only R500,000 is capitalised. The R400,000 research is expensed. The R300,000 between July and October is also expensed — you cannot reinstate it once criteria are later met.

This is the single most mark-losing area in ACC. The past, once expensed, stays expensed. The capitalisation clock only starts on the LATEST date all criteria are met.
